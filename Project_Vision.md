# SF Street Transform — Project Vision

_Last updated: 2026-06-07_

---

## 1. Objective

Develop an interactive, map-based web application that empowers residents, urban advocates, and city planners to visualize and prototype street transformations across San Francisco. The core motivation is to reduce car dependency and re-prioritize public space for people — making streets safer, greener, and more community-centred.

---

## 2. Why This Matters

### Democratization of Public Space
Streets are public property, yet they disproportionately serve car drivers. This project advocates for reimagining those spaces so they benefit all citizens: pedestrians, cyclists, children, the elderly, and anyone who simply wants to enjoy a neighbourhood without the constant threat of traffic.

### Safety
Car crashes remain one of the leading causes of preventable death and injury in San Francisco. Current infrastructure consistently fails to prevent them. This tool lets communities prototype designs that inherently reduce vehicle speeds and create safer environments for vulnerable road users.

### Quality of Life
Car-centric design contributes to noise pollution, air pollution, soil and water contamination, and limits access to quality recreation space — impacts felt most acutely in less-privileged neighbourhoods. Visualising narrower streets, more green space, and community amenities can help residents articulate and advocate for better environments.

### Inspiring Proactive Action
Historically, San Francisco's most significant urban changes have been reactive — triggered by earthquakes or pandemics rather than community vision. This application gives residents a proactive platform: visualise potential improvements, generate data-backed arguments, and drive political action (e.g., in collaboration with groups like Strong Towns SF).

### Economic and Environmental Benefits
Reducing car dependency lowers fuel consumption, emissions, and noise. City-wide paid street parking could generate meaningful revenue for infrastructure, addressing the financial inequity where non-car-owners effectively subsidise free parking for drivers.

---

## 3. Historical Context

San Francisco's urban development has been largely reactive:

- **Post-1989 Earthquake:** Removal of the Embarcadero Freeway demonstrated the city's capacity for transformative change when forced to act.
- **COVID-19 Pandemic:** Temporary closures of the Great Highway and JFK Drive became permanent due to overwhelming public demand. The "Slow Streets" programme expanded. Restaurant outdoor seating was permanently legalised. All of this happened because people experienced what streets *could* be.
- **Vision Zero Challenges:** Despite the Vision Zero initiative, pedestrian fatalities continue. Speed bumps, minor bike lane upgrades, and right-turn barriers are insufficient because they don't fundamentally reduce vehicular volume or speed. Drivers adapt; the infrastructure does not.

## 4. Practical Vision: Suggestions for Change

These are the kinds of street transformations the application is built to prototype and communicate.

### Street Design

1. **Narrower Residential Streets / End Destinations** — Design streets to be narrower or dead-ends rather than through-routes, naturally reducing vehicle speed and discouraging cut-through traffic.
2. **Repurposing Asphalt** — Remove unnecessary pavement from narrowed streets and replace it with community amenities:
   - Community gardens
   - Benches and seating
   - Jungle gyms / exercise stations
   - Mini playgrounds
   - BBQ stations or firewood ovens
   - One dedicated delivery vehicle space per block
3. **One-Way Residential Streets** — Eliminate bidirectional traffic, enhance grid safety, and reduce speeds.
4. **Windy Residential Streets** — Introduce curves to force lower speeds and create opportunities for neighbourhood-friendly infrastructure.
5. **Lower Speed Limits** — 15 mph in residential areas for all vehicles.
6. **Replace Stop Signs with Roundabouts** — Especially on one-way streets; improves flow, reduces noise and pollution from constant stopping and starting.
7. **LED Warning Lights at Pedestrian Crossings** — Increases driver awareness at every crossing.
8. **Elevated Pedestrian Crossings** — Raised to sidewalk level; functions as a natural speed table and improves accessibility for people with mobility issues.

### Parking

9. **Remove Street Parking from Narrow / Residential Streets** — Reclaim space for seating, trees, playgrounds, and gathering areas.
10. **Reconfigure Parking on Higher-Traffic Streets** — Shift parking from the right curb to the centre / left side; creates a cyclist buffer, narrows perceived street width (reducing speeds), and can increase total parking capacity.
11. **Charge for All Street Parking** — Citywide paid parking addresses the subsidy inequity and funds infrastructure.
12. **Remove or Limit Parking Lots** — Reduce underutilised lots (e.g., Embarcadero) to reclaim land for productive urban use.
13. **Dedicated Bicycle Parking** — At all major transit hubs and commuter locations.

### Alternative Transportation

14. **Neighbourhood Public Transit Network** — Local minivan routes for short errands; flexible, convenient, reduces car trips for small distances.
15. **Promote Cargo & Surrey Bikes** — Drop-off points and infrastructure for cargo bikes, surrey bikes, and scooters; makes these alternatives viable for residents of all fitness levels.

## 5. UI/UX Vision (Mockups)

Three mockups illustrate the intended end-state of the application. Reference images: `Mockup 1.png`, `Mockup 2.png`, `Mockup 3.png`.

### Mockup 1 — Broad Application Layout
- "Reimagine SF Streets" header with neighbourhood dropdown and 2D View navigation.
- Left panel: **"Design Your Street"** checklist (Playground, Jungle Gym, Bike Lane Barrier, LED Crosswalk, Community Garden, Mini Roundabout, 15 MPH Zone, etc.).
- Centre: **Before ↔ After drag slider** for a specific street (e.g. South Van Ness Ave).
- Right: **Info Panel** showing Impact / Space / Cost / Longevity / Funding / Reference.
- Button: **"Go to interactive 3D view"** with Rotate/Zoom Controls.

### Mockup 2 — Split View Detail
- Left: **Transformation Menu** with icons for Trees, Bike Lane, Outdoor Seating, Stormwater Planters.
- Right: **Info Panels** showing quantified impact: "15% less noise", "25 new trees", "Improved pedestrian safety".
- Bottom: Perspective tabs — **Pedestrian / Business / Fire Dept**.
- **Counterpoint Mode** toggle to switch between stakeholder perspectives.

### Mockup 3 — Before/After & Sidebar Detail
- Split Before/After street view for South Van Ness Avenue.
- Sidebar with **Tags** and **Long-Term Impact** panel.
- **Transformation Menu** icon row.
- **Counterpoint Toggle** (On/Off).
